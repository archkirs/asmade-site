The reviewer problem

A final file is not enough. A long AI chat is too much.

AI detectors can be wrong. Guessing from the final file can also be unfair.

A short process record can help you understand the work without reading a raw archive first.

How it works

Follow up

What the person did
Shows planning, choices, edits, source decisions, and final approval.

How AI helped
Summarizes the AI role without forcing you to read a full chat first.

Available materials
Lists drafts, notes, files, AI chats, versions, or screenshots.

Careful limits
States what cannot be proven from the record alone.

What to ask for

Ask for a Human Contribution Record.

Instead of asking only “Did you use AI?”, ask the author, student, creator, or worker to provide a short process record.

The record can show

What the person did planning, decisions, edits, and final approval
How AI helped where AI was used and for what purpose
What materials exist drafts, notes, files, versions, and AI chats
What is uncertain what the record cannot prove

Who this helps

For people who receive or check work.

The reviewer does not secretly upload someone else’s file. The reviewer asks the maker for a record.

Possible reviewers

Teachers understand student process when AI use must be explained
Clients understand how freelance work was made
Editors and publishers review disclosure and creative direction
Platforms and employers ask for context instead of guessing

Useful when

The final file is unclear and you need to see the process
AI use is allowed but must be explained and the maker has drafts or AI chats
The raw folder is too large and you need a short summary first
AI detectors are not enough and human judgment is still required

What this is not

A readable process record, not secret checking.

Not secret file checking Not an AI detector Not legal certification Not proof of quality Not copyright proof Not automatic judgment

FAQ

The record must come from the person who made the work.

This is not a tool for secretly checking someone else’s file. It is a readable process record created from materials provided by the person who made the work.

A future version may include a short review code for each record. This pilot does not create a public registry or legal certification system.
